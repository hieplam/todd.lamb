Day six. Somewhere on the Ho Chi Minh Road near Khe Sanh. Rain since morning. I pulled under the awning of a small general store at some kilometre marker I can't remember now. The owner poured me a glass of strong tea — no invitation, no question — as if a motorbike rider stopping in from the rain was just a Tuesday. I sat there drinking it, watching the rain fall on the pine forest, and thought: this is probably the single most representative moment of the whole trip. Nothing grand, nothing postcard-worthy. Just me, alone on a long road, with a glass of tea and the rain.

My first end-to-end ride took 10 days and 1,900 km, mostly on the Ho Chi Minh Road. I avoided National Highway 1A — I don't enjoy straight roads packed with trucks. The real cost — nothing inflated, nothing left out — came to around 6.5 million VND for the 10 days, not counting fuel and wear on the bike.

Below is the day-by-day log: the memorable stretches and the ones I'd skip if I did it again.

## Route and Trip Stats

|              |                                                                   |
| ------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Distance     | 1,900 km                                                          |
| Duration     | 10 days                                                           |
| Bike         | Honda CB500X                                                      |
| Timing       | Late March – early April; light morning rain common in Central Vietnam |

Route: Saigon → Bảo Lộc → Buôn Ma Thuột → Kon Tum → Khe Sanh → Phong Nha → Hà Tĩnh → Ninh Bình → Hanoi.

## Days 1–2: Saigon up to the Central Highlands

Day one to Bảo Lộc, 200 km on National Highway 20. After Bảo Lộc Pass the town's cool air greets you — around 22°C in the middle of dry season, smelling of tea bushes and recent rain. One night in Bảo Lộc: a simple rice meal for 45,000 VND, a guesthouse room for 200,000 VND.

Day two up to Buôn Ma Thuột on the western branch of the Ho Chi Minh Road. This was my favourite stretch of those first two days — wide road, light traffic, running through red basalt highlands covered in coffee plantations as far as you can see. In January or February you'd catch the coffee blossom scent; in November or December the smell of ripe red cherries. In March the harvest was just over, the red earth still slick and shiny but without any particular smell.

## Days 3–4: Kon Tum and the Forest Road

I gave Kon Tum a full day. The old wooden cathedral, the Đăk Bla river bank, roasted tube rice in the evening. That afternoon I sat at the suspension bridge watching Bahnar villagers cross — a bridge of rattan rope and wooden planks, just wide enough for one person, swaying with every step.

Leaving Kon Tum on day four, northward on the Ho Chi Minh Road toward Khe Sanh. This 280 km leg is the longest and most service-free of the whole ride — through Lò Xo Pass (20 km of continuous climbing), through the forests of Quảng Nam and Quảng Trị. No real restaurants from kilometre 50 to kilometre 200, just a handful of general stores selling instant noodles and soft drinks. I carried bread rolls and roasted peanuts — enough for the day.

Lunch eaten sitting on the bike by the side of the road, rain and sun taking turns. I reached Khe Sanh at five in the afternoon, stopped briefly at the Khe Sanh Museum, then found a room. Accommodation is thin around Khe Sanh; guesthouses near the museum run 250,000–350,000 VND.

## Days 5–6: Phong Nha – Kẻ Bàng

This is the section I would spend more time on if I did it again. The Phong Nha – Kẻ Bàng forest along the western Ho Chi Minh Road is so quiet you can hear every revolution of your engine — no other vehicles, no construction sounds, just forest and road and you. Occasionally a gibbon call drifts out from deep in the trees.

One and a half days in Phong Nha: Dark Cave and Phong Nha Cave. Slept at a village guesthouse for 300,000 VND, ate at a stilt-house restaurant for 80,000 VND a meal — better than the tourist places nearby.

![The western Ho Chi Minh Road cutting through the silent Phong Nha forest, tall trees on both sides, not another vehicle in sight](/images/posts/xuyen-viet-10-ngay-sg-hn/scene-2.svg)

## Days 7–8: Hà Tĩnh and the Central Coast Plain

After Phong Nha I dropped onto National Highway 1A at Đồng Hới and rode north to Hà Tĩnh — the only stretch of 1A in the entire trip, and I didn't enjoy it. Heavy traffic, trucks threading past each other, heavier air. I pushed through it in about two hours.

In Hà Tĩnh I stopped at Thiên Cầm, a small beach almost nobody talks about. Room for 250,000 VND, clean beach, grilled fish with the guesthouse family in the evening. They were drinking home rice wine and offered me a glass. I don't drink the stuff, but I took one small sip so as not to be rude.

Day eight through Ninh Bình, arriving at Tràng An at two in the afternoon — I had planned to come in the evening for fewer crowds, but the boats stop running at five. Just enough time to ride around Tam Cốc and climb Hang Múa for the sunset view. Five hundred stone steps; I climbed them all with legs that had 1,600 km in them, but I climbed them.

## Days 9–10: The Finish in Hanoi

Day nine, Ninh Bình to Hanoi is only 90 km, so I rode slowly. Stopped for bún bò Nam Bộ in Phủ Lý (an odd name for a dish from that direction, but the place was genuinely good), then arrived in Hanoi around noon.

Day ten was a buffer day — bike service, laundry, catching up on sleep. Nothing much else. That evening I ate bún chả on Hàng Mành street, sitting alone, mentally retracing every day's road in reverse order from Hanoi all the way back to that first morning in Saigon.

## Practical Notes

- **10-day cost (1,900 km, solo, own bike):** Fuel roughly 800,000 VND (the CB500X drinks about 4 litres per 100 km), food averaging 120,000–150,000 VND per day, budget guesthouses 200,000–350,000 VND per night, entrance fees 200,000 VND total. Grand total excluding the bike: approximately 5.5–6.5 million VND.
- **Stretches worth slowing down for:** The western Ho Chi Minh Road through Phong Nha (top priority), the Central Highlands between Kon Tum and Buôn Ma Thuột, Lò Xo Pass.
- **Stretches to push through:** National Highway 1A from Đồng Hới up to Hà Tĩnh, the urban approach into Buôn Ma Thuột.
- **Pack:** stomach medicine, medicated balm, a 20,000 mAh power bank (no outlets at roadside stalls near Khe Sanh), a plastic bag cover for your backpack.
- **Best season:** January–April or September–November for the full route. July–August brings heavy rain to Central Vietnam and occasional landslides on the western Ho Chi Minh Road.
- **Bike:** The CB500X is ideal for this route — enough torque to climb Lò Xo Pass, a big enough tank to cover 250 km without stopping, and not so heavy you can't pick it up if you drop it.
